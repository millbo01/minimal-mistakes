## How Structure Determines Behavior

The foundational claim of IPF is simple but profound: **the same person in different incentive structures will exhibit radically different behavior.**

This isn't about personality, values, or character. It's about optimization. Humans are prediction machines that continuously estimate expected payoffs and select actions accordingly. Change the payoff landscape, and you change the behavior—predictably, mechanically, without touching anything "inside" the person at all.

---

## The Gym vs. Army Example

Consider the same individual in two contexts:

**At the gym:**
- Shows up inconsistently
- Quits when tired
- Skips difficult exercises
- Progress is slow and sporadic
- No external accountability

**In the army:**
- Shows up daily at 5 AM without fail
- Pushes through exhaustion
- Completes every assigned exercise
- Rapid improvement in fitness
- Severe consequences for non-compliance

Same body. Same nervous system. Same "willpower." Completely different outcomes.

**What changed?**

Not the person's internal traits. The incentive structure changed:

| Dimension | Gym | Army |
|-----------|-----|------|
| **Consequences for non-compliance** | None (paid membership continues) | Severe (punishment, social shame, career damage) |
| **Social accountability** | Anonymous, no one watching | Constant observation by peers and superiors |
| **Time horizon** | Distant, abstract goals ("get fit someday") | Immediate, concrete demands ("right now or face consequences") |
| **Cost of quitting** | Low (minor financial loss) | Catastrophic (discharge, loss of career, social identity) |
| **Reward structure** | Delayed, uncertain, invisible | Immediate, certain, socially visible |

The gym optimizes for comfort—you pay to have the *option* to exercise, not the obligation. Showing up is already enough to feel virtuous. The army optimizes for performance—compliance is mandatory, performance is measured, deviation is punished immediately.

**The person isn't "stronger-willed" in the army. The structure makes non-compliance irrational.**

## What This Reveals About Human Behavior

### 1. Values Are Downstream of Structure

Ask the gym-goer what they value: "Health, discipline, self-improvement." Watch what they do: sporadic effort, frequent skipping, eventual abandonment.

Are they hypocrites? No. They genuinely believe those values matter. But **values don't drive behavior when costs are real—incentives do.**

The army doesn't make soldiers "value discipline more." It makes discipline the only rational strategy given the consequences of non-compliance. The behavior looks the same (consistent exercise), but the mechanism is entirely different.

### 2. Willpower Is Largely a Myth

We attribute the gym-goer's inconsistency to "lack of willpower" and the soldier's consistency to "strong willpower." This is backwards.

**"Willpower" is what we call behavior when we can't see the incentive structure clearly.**

The soldier doesn't need more willpower than the gym-goer. They need less—because the structure does the work. When non-compliance becomes prohibitively costly and compliance becomes socially rewarded, the "choice" isn't difficult. The optimization is obvious.

What we experience as willpower is often just the felt struggle of trying to optimize against misaligned incentives. Remove the misalignment, and the struggle disappears.

### 3. Behavior Predicts Structure Better Than Structure Predicts Values

If you observe behavior consistently, you can infer the underlying incentive structure with high accuracy. But if you only know stated values, you can't predict behavior at all.

- Watch what people **do** under cost → reveals dominant tier and actual incentives
- Listen to what people **say** under comfort → reveals social desirability and identity narratives

Behavior is data. Values are stories we tell about the behavior afterward.

## The Mechanism at Every Scale

This pattern doesn't just apply to individuals. It operates identically at institutional, market, and societal scales.

### Individual Level

Person works late hours, sacrifices family time, optimizes for career advancement. Ask why: "I value success and providing for my family."

Reality: The employment structure rewards visible output and punishes perceived laziness. Promotion depends on perceived dedication. Peers who leave at 5 PM get passed over. Health consequences and relationship strain are delayed, uncertain, and invisible to employers.

The person is optimizing correctly given the incentive landscape. They're not "choosing" to sacrifice family—they're responding rationally to a structure that makes sacrifice profitable and balance costly.

### Institutional Level

University claims to value "rigorous truth-seeking and education." Observe behavior: Prioritizes publication count over replication quality. Rewards flashy findings over careful methodology. Hires based on grant-getting ability rather than teaching excellence. Promotes administrators who increase revenue, not educational outcomes.

Is the institution hypocritical? No. It's optimizing for the incentives it actually faces: funding depends on publication metrics, rankings depend on research output, survival depends on revenue growth. "Education" is the stated mission, but the structure rewards something else entirely.

### Societal Level

Democracy claims to value "informed citizens making collective decisions for long-term wellbeing." Observe behavior: Short-term policies win elections. Long-term investments (infrastructure, education, debt reduction) get deferred. Politicians optimize for media coverage and polling numbers, not outcomes that manifest after they leave office.

Are voters and politicians corrupt? No. They're responding rationally to incentive structures with short time horizons, where visibility matters more than outcomes, and where consequences of bad policy fall on future generations who can't vote today.

## Why This Matters for Diagnosis

Traditional analysis asks: "Why are people making bad choices?"

IPF asks: "What incentives make the 'bad' choice rational?"

**This shift in framing is everything.**

When you diagnose behavior as individual moral failure, you get interventions like:
- Educational campaigns ("teach people to make better choices")
- Moral exhortation ("just try harder")
- Personnel replacement ("fire the bad actors, hire good ones")

These interventions fail consistently because they ignore the incentive structure. The new person faces the same incentives and produces the same behavior.

When you diagnose behavior as structurally rational, you get interventions like:
- **Increase consequences for misaligned behavior** (make exploitation costly)
- **Reduce opacity** (make consequences visible and attributable)
- **Shift time horizons** (make long-term costs immediate)
- **Change reward structures** (make aligned behavior profitable)

These interventions work because they change what's rational.

## The "Same Structure, Same Behavior" Principle

IPF's core predictive claim:

> **Identical incentive structures produce identical behavior patterns, regardless of who the actors are.**

This is testable and falsifiable. If you map the incentive structure correctly:
- You can predict aggregate behavior before it occurs
- You can explain persistent patterns that traditional models call "irrational"
- You can design interventions that reliably change outcomes

**Corollary:** If behavior isn't changing despite "good people" and "best intentions," your incentive structure hasn't actually changed.

## Three Common Objections (And Why They're Wrong)

### Objection 1: "But people have agency! They can choose!"

**Response:** Yes, people choose. But choice is the *output* of the optimization process, not an input independent of incentives.

You're free to choose among the options you perceive as available, weighted by the consequences you expect. Change the perceived options or expected consequences, and the "choice" changes automatically.

Agency is real. It just operates *within* the incentive terrain, not outside it. The terrain determines which choices are rational.

### Objection 2: "This is too cynical. It reduces everything to self-interest."

**Response:** It's not cynicism—it's mechanics. And it's not "self-interest" in the narrow financial sense.

The six tiers include kinship (protecting family), belonging (maintaining social bonds), meaning (pursuing purpose), and stewardship (serving collective good). These are genuine human motivations—they just operate according to incentive logic like everything else.

The framework doesn't claim people are selfish. It claims people are optimizers. What they optimize *for* depends on which tier is dominant. Change the tier activation, change the optimization target.

### Objection 3: "What about people who act against their self-interest?"

**Response:** This usually means one of two things:

1. **You're modeling the wrong tier.** What looks like acting "against self-interest" (sacrificing income for a cause) is actually optimizing a higher-weighted tier (meaning, principle, belonging). The behavior is still rational given their tier structure.

2. **You're confusing stated vs revealed preference.** They *say* they want X, but consistently choose Y under cost. Their revealed preference is Y. They're optimizing correctly—you're just listening to the wrong signal.

Genuine self-sabotage exists (addiction, mental illness, trauma responses), but it's rarer than people think. Most "irrational" behavior is rational within the actor's perceived incentive structure and tier weights.

## Practical Application: Analyzing Any System

When you encounter persistent dysfunction, follow this diagnostic:

### Step 1: Map the Behavior
What are people actually doing? Not what they say they're doing—what do their actions reveal?

### Step 2: Infer the Incentives
What payoff structure makes that behavior rational? 
- What gets rewarded?
- What gets punished?
- What consequences are visible vs hidden?
- What time horizons dominate decisions?

### Step 3: Identify the Gap
What's the difference between:
- Stated goals (what the system claims to optimize for)
- Revealed goals (what behavior actually optimizes for)

That gap reveals the misalignment.

### Step 4: Locate the Leverage Point
Where could you change incentives to make aligned behavior rational?
- Increase transparency (make hidden consequences visible)
- Shift time horizons (make long-term costs immediate)
- Change reward structures (make aligned behavior profitable)
- Remove perverse incentives (stop rewarding misaligned behavior)

### Step 5: Predict the Outcome
If you implemented the structural change, what behavior would become rational? Does that behavior align with stated goals?

If yes: Implement and measure.  
If no: Redesign until alignment emerges.

## The Gym Problem at Scale

The gym example scales perfectly to institutions and societies:

**The Gym Model (Misaligned):**
- People pay for access but don't use it
- No consequences for non-participation
- Delayed, uncertain benefits
- Everyone optimizes for comfort, not performance
- Predictable result: Most people don't achieve fitness goals

**The Army Model (Aligned):**
- Participation is mandatory, not optional
- Immediate consequences for non-compliance
- Clear, measurable standards
- Everyone optimizes for performance because that's what survives
- Predictable result: Nearly everyone achieves fitness standards

Most institutions operate on the Gym Model—they create the appearance of accountability without actual consequences. They measure inputs (did you show up?) instead of outputs (did you produce results?). They reward compliance with process instead of achievement of purpose.

Then they wonder why performance is mediocre despite "good people" and "clear goals."

**The problem isn't the people. It's that you're running a gym and expecting army-level performance.**
